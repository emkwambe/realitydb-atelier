## A1. Naming correction — sixth dimension

**Supersedes SOT §1.5**

The sixth dimension is renamed from "AI-Augmented Knowledge Worker"
to **Augmented Intelligence**.

Reason: the dimension names a *capability*, not a *person type*.
"Augmented Intelligence" sits grammatically parallel to the other five
dimensions and keeps the business acumen grain intact — it describes
judgment amplified by tools, not a category of worker or a skills track.

Updated canonical naming rule (add to SOT §8):

| Use this | Never use this |
|---|---|
| Augmented Intelligence (dimension name) | AI-Augmented Knowledge Worker (dimension name) |
| AI-Augmented Knowledge Worker (persona description) | AI skills, AI readiness, AI training |

Note: "AI-Augmented Knowledge Worker" remains valid as a *persona
description* (the type of professional Atelier develops). It is not
valid as a *dimension name* inside the six-dimension framework.

## A2. The six dimensions — theoretical grounding and content reference

**Supersedes SOT §1.6**

Each dimension below includes: the canonical name, what it measures in
Atelier's context, the theoretical tradition it draws from, foundational
reference works, and a content anchor — the one question that frames
every exercise, briefing prompt, and Boardroom round in that dimension.

---

### Dimension 1 — Financial Intelligence

**What it measures in Atelier:**
The learner's ability to interpret financial signals, quantify the
impact of a business crisis, connect operational data to unit economics,
and communicate financial trade-offs clearly to a non-finance audience.

**Theoretical tradition:**
Management accounting, corporate finance, and financial statement
analysis. The applied branch — not academic finance theory — grounded
in how practitioners read P&L, balance sheets, and cash flow to make
operating decisions.

**Foundational references:**
- Penman, S. (2013). *Financial Statement Analysis and Security
  Valuation.* McGraw-Hill. — The standard on reading financial
  statements as a practitioner.
- Kaplan, R. & Atkinson, A. (1998). *Advanced Management Accounting.*
  Prentice Hall. — Unit economics, cost behavior, and managerial
  decision-making under constraints.
- Brigham, E. & Houston, J. (2021). *Fundamentals of Financial
  Management.* Cengage. — ROI, IRR, breakeven, capital allocation
  in plain language.
- Ittelson, T. (2009). *Financial Statements: A Step-by-Step Guide.*
  Career Press. — The most accessible entry point for non-finance
  professionals.

**Atelier expression:**
ClearBank (AML structuring pattern buried in 18 months of wire
transactions), MedCore (net collection rate drop — four points
that represent millions in unrealized revenue).

**The content anchor question:**
*"Does this finding change how money moves through the business —
and by how much?"*

### Dimension 2 — Strategic Intelligence

**What it measures in Atelier:**
The learner's ability to situate a data finding in competitive context,
identify the long-term implications of a short-term operational pattern,
reason about market dynamics, and frame a recommendation that serves
the organization's strategic position — not just fixes the immediate
problem.

**Theoretical tradition:**
Strategic management and competitive strategy. The applied branch —
how practitioners use strategic frameworks to evaluate decisions, not
academic IO economics.

**Foundational references:**
- Porter, M. (1980). *Competitive Strategy.* Free Press. — Five
  Forces, generic strategies, the foundational competitive analysis
  framework. Every strategy dimension rubric traces back here.
- Porter, M. (1985). *Competitive Advantage.* Free Press. — Value
  chain analysis. Where value is created or destroyed in operations.
- Rumelt, R. (2011). *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy.* Crown Business.
  — The clearest modern treatment of what strategic thinking actually
  means in practice vs. strategic theater.
- Mintzberg, H., Ahlstrand, B. & Lampel, J. (1998). *Strategy Safari.*
  Free Press. — The ten schools of strategic thought; useful for
  understanding why learners reach for different frames.
- Kim, W.C. & Mauborgne, R. (2005). *Blue Ocean Strategy.* Harvard
  Business School Press. — Value innovation and market-creation
  thinking; essential for the recommendation axis.

**Atelier expression:**
The CEO briefing recommendation axis. A learner who finds the churn
spike but recommends a tactical fix without addressing the competitive
position that created it is demonstrating weak strategic intelligence
regardless of their query skill.

**The content anchor question:**
*"What does this finding tell us about where the organization is
winning, losing, or about to lose — and what should it do differently?"*

### Dimension 3 — Operational Intelligence

**What it measures in Atelier:**
The learner's ability to understand how work actually flows through an
organization — where processes break, where dependencies create
concentration risk, where efficiency losses compound, and how
operational data reveals system behavior that financial data obscures.

**Theoretical tradition:**
Operations management, supply chain theory, and systems thinking.
The practitioner branch — how organizations execute at scale, where
they fail, and how data surfaces those failures.

**Foundational references:**
- Goldratt, E. & Cox, J. (1984). *The Goal.* North River Press. —
  Theory of constraints. The foundational text on identifying and
  managing operational bottlenecks.
- Womack, J. & Jones, D. (1996). *Lean Thinking.* Simon & Schuster.
  — Value stream mapping, waste identification, flow optimization.
  Essential for supply chain and operations modules.
- Senge, P. (1990). *The Fifth Discipline.* Doubleday. — Systems
  thinking as a management discipline. Causal loops, feedback,
  unintended consequences in complex operations.
- Chopra, S. & Meindl, P. (2015). *Supply Chain Management.*
  Pearson. — The standard academic reference for supply chain
  structure, risk, and resilience.
- Slack, N., Chambers, S. & Johnston, R. (2010). *Operations
  Management.* Pearson. — Comprehensive practitioner reference
  for process design, capacity, and quality.

**Atelier expression:**
SupplyLink (COGS up 11 points, on-time delivery down 12 —
a supplier concentration problem hiding in procurement data),
TowerNet (regional churn spike tracing to a network SLA failure
in operational metrics).

**The content anchor question:**
*"Where in the system did this break, and what does that reveal
about how the organization is actually running vs. how it thinks
it's running?"*

---

### Dimension 4 — Decision Intelligence

**What it measures in Atelier:**
The learner's ability to make sound judgments under ambiguity —
evaluating trade-offs, reasoning with incomplete information,
identifying risk, prioritizing under constraints, and updating
judgment when new evidence surfaces. The dimension that the
Boardroom is specifically designed to test.

**Theoretical tradition:**
Behavioral decision theory, judgment and decision-making (JDM)
research, and applied risk analysis. The critical insight from
this literature: most poor business decisions fail not because
of missing data but because of systematic cognitive errors in
how available data is interpreted.

**Foundational references:**
- Kahneman, D. (2011). *Thinking, Fast and Slow.* Farrar, Straus
  and Giroux. — System 1 vs. System 2 thinking. The foundational
  text on cognitive biases that distort business judgment. Every
  epistemic honesty rubric criterion maps to a bias named here.
- Kahneman, D., Sibony, O. & Sunstein, C. (2021). *Noise: A Flaw
  in Human Judgment.* Little, Brown. — Variability in judgment
  across people and situations. Directly relevant to why two
  analysts reading the same data reach different conclusions.
- Russo, J. & Schoemaker, P. (2002). *Winning Decisions.* Currency
  Doubleday. — Applied framework for structuring decisions under
  uncertainty. The practitioner companion to Kahneman.
- Hammond, J., Keeney, R. & Raiffa, H. (1998). *Smart Choices.*
  Harvard Business School Press. — Decision framing, trade-off
  analysis, and risk evaluation in practice.
- Taleb, N. (2007). *The Black Swan.* Random House. — The limits
  of prediction and the cost of epistemic overconfidence.
  Directly relevant to the epistemic honesty axis.

**Atelier expression:**
The Boardroom. Three adversarial AI directors (CFO, general
counsel, independent director) who read the learner's briefing
and SQL trail and challenge the recommendation. The CFO questions
whether the financial projection is defensible. The GC asks about
disclosure obligations. The independent director challenges
whether the causal inference is sound. Learner has 90 seconds
per response. Updating judgment under pressure — or failing to —
is the data point.

**The content anchor question:**
*"What would you need to believe for this recommendation to be
wrong — and have you considered it?"*

### Dimension 5 — Communication & Influence Intelligence

**What it measures in Atelier:**
The learner's ability to translate a data finding into a message
a non-technical executive can act on — structuring the narrative,
choosing what to include and exclude, calibrating confidence
appropriately, and framing for a specific audience's decision
context. The CEO briefing is the primary expression of this
dimension.

**Theoretical tradition:**
Business communication, rhetoric, and stakeholder theory.
The practitioner branch — how professionals make their thinking
legible to decision-makers who do not share their technical
context.

**Foundational references:**
- Minto, B. (1987). *The Pyramid Principle.* Minto International.
  — The foundational framework for structured executive
  communication. Every good CEO briefing follows SCQA (Situation,
  Complication, Question, Answer) whether the writer knows it or
  not. Atelier's briefing rubric is grounded here.
- Duarte, N. (2010). *Resonate.* Wiley. — Narrative structure in
  business communication. What makes a briefing land vs. what
  makes it ignored.
- Freeman, R.E. (1984). *Strategic Management: A Stakeholder
  Approach.* Pitman. — Stakeholder theory. Who is affected by
  a decision, what they care about, and how that shapes what a
  briefing must address.
- Heath, C. & Heath, D. (2007). *Made to Stick.* Random House.
  — Why some ideas are remembered and others forgotten.
  The SUCCESs framework (Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible,
  Emotional, Stories) maps directly onto briefing quality.
- Cialdini, R. (1984). *Influence.* William Morrow. — The
  psychology of persuasion. What makes a recommendation credible
  to a skeptical audience.

**Atelier expression:**
The CEO briefing deliverable itself. The 5-axis rubric's
"recommendation specificity" and "epistemic honesty" axes both
live in this dimension — a briefing that is technically accurate
but communicated without appropriate confidence calibration
fails this dimension even if it passes the others.

The Boardroom's "communication clarity" component (5% of
AcumenIQ score) is also this dimension — can the learner explain
their reasoning to a skeptical director without either
over-reaching or retreating?

**The content anchor question:**
*"If the CEO read only the first two sentences of your briefing
and acted on them, would they make the right call?"*

### Dimension 6 — Augmented Intelligence

**What it measures in Atelier:**
The learner's ability to use AI tools as judgment amplifiers
without surrendering judgment to them — knowing when to trust
an AI output, when to challenge it, how to prompt for useful
analysis rather than plausible-sounding hallucination, and how
to maintain epistemic ownership of a conclusion that AI
contributed to. The dimension that ensures business acumen
remains a human capability in an AI-augmented environment.

**Theoretical tradition:**
Human-computer interaction (HCI), cognitive systems engineering,
and the emerging literature on human-AI collaboration. The
critical insight: AI does not distribute its benefits equally.
Professionals with strong underlying judgment extract more value
from AI than professionals with weak judgment — because they
know when the AI is wrong.

**Foundational references:**
- Noy, S. & Zhang, W. (2023). "Experimental Evidence on the
  Productivity Effects of Generative AI." *MIT Working Paper.*
  — The foundational empirical study showing ChatGPT raises
  average performance but compresses the distribution. The
  implication: strong human judgment remains the differentiator.
- Brynjolfsson, E., Li, D. & Raymond, L. (2023). "Generative AI
  at Work." *NBER Working Paper 31161.* — AI as a skill
  compressor in customer service. The workers it helps most
  are the ones who needed it least.
- Kahneman, D., Sibony, O. & Sunstein, C. (2021). *Noise.*
  Little, Brown. — AI reduces some noise but introduces new
  forms of systematic error. Human oversight remains essential.
- Licklider, J.C.R. (1960). "Man-Computer Symbiosis."
  *IRE Transactions on Human Factors in Electronics.* — The
  original articulation of augmented intelligence: computers
  should augment human intellect, not replace it. Still the
  clearest statement of the principle.
- Engelbart, D. (1962). "Augmenting Human Intellect: A
  Conceptual Framework." *Stanford Research Institute.*
  — The foundational paper on intelligence augmentation.
  The intellectual ancestor of every AI-augmented tool.

**Atelier expression:**
The Co-Pilot ("Stuck?" button — Socratic questions only,
never SQL). The Boardroom AI directors (Claude playing
adversarial roles — learner must hold their reasoning against
AI pressure, not defer to it). Over-reliance on Co-Pilot
is logged and visible to the grader. A learner who cannot
defend their recommendation against an AI director has not
demonstrated Augmented Intelligence — they have demonstrated
AI dependence.

**The content anchor question:**
*"If the AI tool you used to reach this conclusion were wrong,
would you have caught it — and how?"*

## A3. The AcumenIQ scoring framework — aligned to the six dimensions

The eight AcumenIQ rubric components from the research report
map to the six dimensions as follows. This alignment is what
makes Atelier Rank meaningful across dimensions rather than
as a single opaque number.

| AcumenIQ component | Weight | Primary dimension | Secondary dimension |
|---|---|---|---|
| Problem framing | 20% | Decision Intelligence | Strategic Intelligence |
| Commercial reasoning | 20% | Financial Intelligence | Operational Intelligence |
| Trade-off judgment | 15% | Decision Intelligence | Financial Intelligence |
| Option quality | 10% | Strategic Intelligence | Decision Intelligence |
| Risk and uncertainty management | 10% | Decision Intelligence | Augmented Intelligence |
| Execution realism | 10% | Operational Intelligence | Strategic Intelligence |
| Learning agility and calibration | 10% | Augmented Intelligence | Decision Intelligence |
| Communication clarity | 5% | Communication & Influence | All dimensions |

This mapping means Atelier Rank can report not just a composite
score but a dimensional profile:

> "Your Financial Intelligence is at the 84th percentile.
> Your Augmented Intelligence is at the 41st percentile.
> The gap suggests strong quantitative reasoning that may
> be undermined by over-reliance on AI outputs in the
> analysis phase."

That profile is what makes the rank a career signal, not just
a leaderboard position.

## A4. The epistemic honesty axis — special treatment

Epistemic honesty is weighted 2× in Atelier Rank calculation.
It is not a standalone dimension — it runs through all six.

| Dimension | What epistemic honesty means in that dimension |
|---|---|
| Financial Intelligence | Not overstating the precision of financial projections. "Revenue could recover 8–12% under this scenario" not "Revenue will recover 10%." |
| Strategic Intelligence | Acknowledging competitive factors that complicate the recommendation, not just those that support it. |
| Operational Intelligence | Naming the operational assumptions the analysis depends on, especially those the data cannot verify. |
| Decision Intelligence | Stating what would need to be true for the recommendation to be wrong. (Kahneman: pre-mortem thinking.) |
| Communication & Influence | Calibrating confidence language to evidence strength. Not performing certainty to sound authoritative. |
| Augmented Intelligence | Flagging where AI contributed to the analysis and where the human judgment overrode or verified it. |

The learner who writes "Based on this data, I am highly confident
that..." when the data supports three plausible explanations is
failing epistemic honesty across four dimensions simultaneously.
That is why it is weighted 2×. Overconfident briefings are the
most common failure mode in real executive communication — and
the hardest to teach without a rubric that names it explicitly.
